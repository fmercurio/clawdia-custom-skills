# Business-Model Refinement

Use this reference when the context-to-spec workflow is applied to a business model, value proposition, or commercial offer.

## Required distinctions

Never collapse these layers:

1. **Mechanism** — internal capability that creates value (e.g. AI memory, knowledge graph, methodology).
2. **Feature** — what the product does.
3. **Deliverable** — what the customer tangibly receives.
4. **Benefit** — how the deliverable improves the customer's situation.
5. **Observable outcome** — measurable behavior or business effect.
6. **Commercial promise** — concise claim the offer can responsibly make.

A mechanism is rarely the strongest promise. Translate it into an outcome without promising effects outside the product's control.

## Refinement sequence

### 1. Identify the economic buyer

Ask who signs and pays, not only who uses. Compare individual, corporate, sponsored, and service-assisted models when uncertain. The first buyer determines acquisition, onboarding, proof, permissions, and retention.

### 2. Select one urgent job

Generate genuinely different jobs, such as organization, opportunity activation, capability development, risk reduction, or strategic advice. Select the job with the strongest combination of urgency, willingness to pay, early proof, and fit with available evidence.

### 3. Separate activation from recurrence

For subscription models, identify:

- **Activation deliverable:** first concrete, memorable proof of value.
- **Recurring loop:** repeated behavior and outcome that justify renewal.

Reject a subscription thesis if all useful value is delivered once and no natural recurrence exists.

### 4. Define a time-bound transformation

Ask what the customer should truthfully say after a meaningful interval (often 30, 60, or 90 days). Convert abstract improvement into evidence: actions executed, conversations initiated, risks avoided, cycle time reduced, qualified opportunities created, or capabilities demonstrated.

### 5. Bound the promise

Distinguish:

- outputs the product controls;
- outcomes it influences;
- external results it cannot guarantee.

Do not guarantee revenue, hiring, investment, sales, or third-party decisions unless the business truly controls them and has evidence.

### 6. Build the value chain

Express the causal chain:

```text
customer input → interpretation → prioritized recommendation → action → observed response → learning → next cycle
```

Every weak transition becomes an assumption or experiment.

### 7. Refine the beachhead by transition stage

A demographic or job title is usually not a usable beachhead. Also determine the customer's moment:

- planning a transition;
- recently transitioned and still defining the offer;
- offer defined but no first customers;
- early customers acquired but no repeatable portfolio.

The stage can change the product boundary. A customer who has no offer cannot yet use an opportunity map effectively; the value chain may need to become:

```text
interpret proven experience → formulate 2–3 offer theses → map access → validate → sell → evolve the offer
```

Do not silently broaden into generic career coaching. Set a boundary such as: transform evidenced experience into commercially testable hypotheses, rather than teach a new profession. Treat the chosen stage as a prioritized hypothesis until interviews and behavior validate it.

### 8. Separate validators from buyers

When a customer's network is used to validate an offer, do not treat every relationship as a lead.

- **Validators:** assess problem, language, credibility, alternatives, and fit. Do not disguise a sales pitch as research.
- **Potential buyers:** have plausible need, context, influence, urgency, and ability to buy; the conversation may advance commercially.
- **Connectors:** can create trusted access but need not validate or buy.

A person may change roles when new evidence appears, but record the initial intent and transition. This protects trust, improves metrics, and prevents inflated pipeline claims.

For an unvalidated offer, prefer 2–3 evidence-backed theses over one AI-declared “correct” offer. Each thesis should state customer, problem, proposed outcome, delivery form, authority evidence, assumptions, limits, economic causal chain, accessible validators/buyers, and signals that would justify promotion to the primary offer.

## Stress-test lenses

Use the strongest 3–5 only:

- **Data sufficiency:** Can useful value be produced from what customers will actually provide?
- **Novelty:** Is the output non-obvious or merely polished common sense?
- **Behavior:** Will the customer act, or is the problem execution rather than information?
- **Trust:** Will the customer share sensitive inputs and trust recommendations?
- **Attribution:** Can the customer see the product's contribution to the outcome?
- **Recurrence:** Does value compound or expire after the first deliverable?
- **Scalability:** Is pilot value secretly produced by expensive human work?
- **Positioning:** Will buyers compare it to a cheaper generic category?

## Minimum commercial experiment

Prefer a concierge or assisted pilot before full automation when the core uncertainty is value rather than technical feasibility. Define:

- narrow participant profile;
- duration;
- initial deliverable;
- recurring cadence;
- actions and outcomes recorded;
- willingness-to-pay test using real money or a concrete purchase decision;
- pass/fail thresholds;
- manual steps to measure for future automation.

Interest, compliments, and stated willingness to pay are weak evidence. Execution, renewal, payment, and observed outcomes are stronger.

## Session-derived example pattern

A professional-intelligence product was initially framed as a “digital professional brain.” Refinement produced a stronger hierarchy:

- mechanism: progressive professional intelligence;
- activation deliverable: map of underused assets and commercial connections;
- recurring loop: prioritize, act, track, learn, and remap;
- influenced outcome: qualified conversations and opportunities from the existing network;
- bounded promise: reveal and guide promising moves, not guarantee closed revenue.

This pattern generalizes to products whose technology is compelling but whose commercial promise is initially abstract.
